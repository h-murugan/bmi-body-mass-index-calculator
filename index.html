What your BMI result means

A BMI range of 18.5 to 24.9 is generally considered a healthy weight. Keeping your weight within this range can help reduce your chances of facing health issues later on, like obesity and type 2 diabetes. It’s wonderful to aim for a balanced diet that’s lower in fat and sugar while adding in lots of delicious fruits and veggies. And let’s not forget the importance of staying active! Try to find time for about 30 minutes of physical activity most days of the week to keep your body happy and healthy.

Healthy eating

Eating well not only helps you maintain a healthy weight but also works wonders for preventing diseases, improving digestion, boosting your immune system, and enhancing your mental clarity and mood.

Regular exercise

Regular exercise is a fantastic way to keep fit, manage your weight, lift your spirits, and lower the risk of disease, all contributing to a healthier and longer life.

Adequate sleep

Getting enough sleep is essential for mental clarity, emotional balance, and physical health, helping you feel restored and rejuvenated.

Limitations of BMI

While BMI is a useful tool for assessing healthy weight, it's important to remember that it doesn't apply perfectly to everyone. Some groups may want to take a closer look at what their BMI results really mean, and in some cases, it might not be the best measure to rely on.

Gender

When it comes to kids, it's important to remember that their body composition changes as they grow, so we need to consider their age and gender when looking at their BMI.

Age

As we age, it's common to see increases in body fat and some loss of muscle, which means that BMI might not give a true picture of body fat levels for older adults.

Muscle

Since BMI doesn’t distinguish between muscle and fat, it can sometimes mistakenly label fit individuals as overweight or obese.

Pregnancy

For expectant mothers, weight gain is completely normal as their baby grows. Keeping a healthy BMI before pregnancy is wise and helps reduce potential health risks for both mom and baby.

Race

It's also worth noting that certain health issues may affect some individuals of Black and Asian backgrounds at lower BMI levels than others. If you have questions or concerns, it's always a good idea to chat with your GP or practice nurse for guidance.
